Master Thesis

Inequality is not a failure of the system — it is the system operating exactly as preference designed it. When humans choose under constraint, they default to the familiar. That default clusters. The gap between what is chosen and what is not is stable, measurable, and replicable across menus, ballots, hiring screens, and history. Cultural anthropology names that gap. Statistics measures it. Design is the only tool that moves it.

Stats Problem 1 — Is the Offer Rate Statistically Significant?

Given 87 applications and 2 offers, is the 2.3% offer rate significantly below the expected rate for qualified hospitality applicants?
Given: n = 87, x = 2, p̂ = 2/87 = 0.023
Null hypothesis rate: p₀ = 0.075 (industry baseline for qualified applicants)

Z = (p̂ − p₀) / √(p₀ · (1−p₀) / n)
Z = (0.023 − 0.075) / √(0.075 × 0.925 / 87)
Z = (−0.052) / √(0.000798)
Z = (−0.052) / 0.02825
Z ≈ −1.84
At α = 0.05, critical value = ±1.96
Z = −1.84 → Borderline significant
One additional rejection confirms H₁ at the 95% confidence level.
The offer rate is not random. It is structurally suppressed.

Stats Problem 2 — Callback Discrimination Ratio

Is the callback gap between white-named and Black-named applicants statistically different from 1.0 (no discrimination)?
DR = P(callback | white name) / P(callback | Black name)
DR = 1.50 (Bertrand & Mullainathan 2004, N = 1,300 job ads)

White applicants: ~1 callback per 10 resumes
Black applicants: ~1 callback per 15 resumes

Quillian et al. 2017 meta-analysis (1989–2017):
DR trend coefficient ≈ 0.000 (no significant decline)
p-value > 0.05 → Cannot reject H₀ that discrimination is constant
DR = 1.50 unchanged since 1989 (28 years of data)
This is not noise. This is a structural constant.
C in K(t) = 1.50 for the labor domain.
