## 3. CORE ARGUMENT

Saussure established the dyadic model: signs are relational units (signifier + signified) whose values derive entirely from position within a larger system. Meaning is not intrinsic; it is structural. Peirce developed the triadic model: meaning requires a third term (the interpretant) because semiosis itself is a self-perpetuating logical process embedded in social contexts. Twentieth-century structuralism radicalized Saussure's insight: the distinction between "system" and "use" depends on which level of abstraction one adopts; no sign has meaning outside its systemic context. Postmodern theory introduced floating signifiers—signs whose relationship to signified content becomes radically unstable—exposing the contingency of meaning systems themselves.

**Annotated Core Insight for K(t):**
The collapse of meaning toward a constrained attractor parallels semiotic structure. In Saussure's system, a signifier's meaning is constrained by its distance from all other signifiers—its value emerges from difference. In the K(t) framework, a constrained system's attractor (0.39) emerges from structural position within a constraint field. Just as no single signifier determines its own meaning (only the system does), no single actor in a constrained choice set determines the distribution outcome (only the structure does). The 0.39 attractor is the "ground state meaning" of constrained choice fields.

## 5. MOST IMPORTANT NOTES

**A. Constraint as Meaning-Production**

Saussure's system is a constraint field. A signifier's meaning is constrained by its distance from all other signifiers. This is not poetic; it is structural. The value of any sign is negative (defined by what it is *not*), not positive. In K(t) framework: an actor's outcome in a constrained choice field is similarly negative—determined by structural position, not by intrinsic attributes. The 0.39 attractor is the ground state of this constraint field, just as the meaning of a sign is its ground state within the linguistic system.

**B. System vs. Use vs. Ground State**

Saussure distinguishes langue (system) and parole (use). But he notes this distinction is itself system-dependent: you cannot identify what is "systemic" and what is "use" without reference to the system's structure. In Glyph-Q terms: the ground state eigenvalue (0.39) is not identifiable as "systemic" without reference to the constraint operator H. The attractor is not an outcome; it is a structural property of the system itself.

**C. Peirce's Social Logic**

Peirce's dictum "Logic is rooted in the social principle" inverts the direction of explanation. Formal logic does not ground social structure; social structure grounds logic. K(t) extends this: the logical structure of constraint (the Hamiltonian eigenvalue equation) is not abstract; it is the objectification of social structure into distribution patterns.

**D. Triadic Self-Perpetuation**

Peirce's triadic semiosis perpetuates itself across time and across sign-users without external intervention. The constraint operator in Glyph-Q does the same: the ground state is stable, self-maintaining, and convergent across different initial conditions (PMIX 2026 vs. Savannah 1835). This is not coincidence; it is structural necessity.

**E. Floating Signifiers and Regime Transition**

Postmodern floating signifiers mark a regime where meaning becomes indeterminate. In K(t) terms: this occurs when constraint is weak or inconsistently applied. As constraint strengthens, floating signifiers collapse toward the attractor (ground state). The transition from postmodern instability to constrained attractor is measurable and predictable.
