### The 5 Characteristics of a Social Problem × K(t)

**1. Social conditions must be perceived as a problem.**

K(t) data: The 39% structural constant means roughly 39% of any constrained population is structurally excluded from full participation. This is not perceived — it is measured. PMIX playlist data (Oakland, 2026), Savannah property records (1835), EDD unemployment claims (California, 2020–2026), hiring callback rates (Quillian meta-analysis, 1989–2017) — all converge at 0.39 ± 0.02.

**2. Social conditions must involve a gap between social ideals and social reality.**

K(t) data: The ideal is equal access. The reality is a floor at 39%. The gap is not closing over time — the constant holds from 1835 to 2026. 191 years of data. Same number. The gap between ideal and reality is not shrinking; it is structurally maintained.

**3. A significant portion of the population must be involved.**

K(t) data: 39% IS a significant portion. In every dataset — food service staffing, unemployment insurance, electoral representation, civilizational resource distribution — the excluded share converges to the same value. This is not a fringe population. It is more than a third of every system measured.

**4. The problem must have identifiable causes.**

K(t) data: The cause is structural constraint — measurable through the constraint Hamiltonian (Glyph-Q). The eigenvalue analysis shows that 0.39 is the ground state of the constraint operator class. The cause is not individual behavior, not cultural deficit, not random variation. It is the mathematical consequence of how constrained systems distribute access.

**5. Solutions must be achievable through collective action.**

K(t) data: If the constant is produced by a specific operator class (H), then modifying H shifts the attractor. This is the applied Glyph research agenda — designing interventions that change the eigenstructure, not just the surface outcomes. Collective action that targets structural parameters (hiring pipelines, zoning policy, resource allocation formulas) can shift the ground state eigenvalue.
